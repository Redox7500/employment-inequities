Data Analysis

Many models fit my data with a very high R2 score, but a linear model predicted much more accurately than any other one. According to the quadratic model, the employment-population ratios in 2035 for white males, white females, Black/African American males, Black/African American females, Asian males, Asian females, Hispanic/Latino males, and Hispanic/Latino females, respectively, would be 64.1, 55, 65.9, 44.3, 65.2, 65.2, 65, and 64.5.

My linear models for white males, white females, Black/African American males, Black/African American females, Asian males, Asian females, Hispanic/Latino males, and Hispanic/Latina females, respectively, are -0.0291x+67.6 (slow decrease), -0.00824x+56 (slow decrease), 0.00879x+64.8 (slow increase), -0.129x+59.8 (faster decrease), 0.0198x+62.8 (slow increase), 0.0198x+62.8, -0.0978x+76.7 (slow decrease), and 0.0495x+58.6 (slow increase). The starting points are 67.5, 56.2, 65.1, 59.8, 63, 63, 76.2, and 58.9, which means those were the employment-population ratios in April of 2024 in the context of this data.

My employment-population ratio for race/ethnicity and gender can be best modeled by a linear function (not in terms of R2, but for much more accurate predictions). The Google Sheets data can be found here. I know this model fits my data best because I tried all of the other available equations and the linear model had the most reasonable predictions.
